5 Socialization Tips For Exchange Students

By Xavier Selman query_builder 2w ago
chat 23 comments thumb_up 1 489 likes


Flying abroad to study is a dream for a lot of students but can easily turn into a living nightmare to those who are intimidated by novelty. We don’t blame you. As an exchange student, you’re probably overwhelmed by what you see and hear. We get it: you’re not used to the unfamiliarity of the environment.

It doesn’t have to be this way. A foreign exchange program can give you some of the best university-related experiences you’ll ever have. You’ll meet a ton of cool people from different cultures and understand the world from their perspective. You’ll go to places you never knew existed and eat food you’ve never thought of tasting. All it takes is one small step towards confidence.

Getting yourself familiar with the people is a good way to get started. Listed below are five socialization tips for even the most introverted foreign students:

1.) Start with your host family

Easing into a new environment becomes a smoother transition when you have people to help you out. That’s where host families come in. The host family is your primary introduction to local culture (provided that they are locals, which they usually are!)

Simple moments like preparing food or running errands together can already kick start your socialization process. Because you know host families have volunteered to have you there, you can be sure that they are sincere in helping you become more familiar with your environment. Small trips to the local market or their favorite restaurant can mean the difference between a fulfilling exchange trip and a lonely one.

2.) Forget what you think you know about people

Visiting another country as a student can feel surreal, and for all the right reasons. You are on your own in another country, socializing with people you have never met before. In order for this to be successful, one of the things you should immediately do is to forget what you think you know about people.

No one wants to be seen as a stereotype. Archetypes and generalizations can only limit the way you understand a culture. This can easily affect the way you subconsciously treat people. Forget stereotypes. Build up your own opinions from your personal experiences.

3.) Ask a lot of questions

It’s never easy to start a conversation with statements, so why not try by asking questions? Even to the shyest of exchange students, asking a question will feel like a natural impulse. Think of it as a door to a better conversation. A simple “what are we doing today in class?” already serves as a good conversation opener. \

A good thing about questions is that it is one of the least awkward forms of socialization because it is not presumptuous in nature. As long as you stick with objective, non-personal questions to start the conversation, you’ll find yourself inching closer and closer to a better conversation topic sooner or later.

4.) Say thank you when you can

Isn’t it frustrating when language barriers limit the way you communicate with people? Knowing an entirely different language is not the sole way to connect with those around you. If you can’t speak in their native tongue (and no one is expecting you to, really), go for the universal language of gratitude.

Say thank you when you can to let your peers know that you appreciate them. Because gratitude is self-explanatory, you can be sure that small acts of kindness (such as appreciation) will go a long way, whether or not said in a specific language.

5.) Go online

If real-life groups in university are too intimidating, you can always start by looking for online groups. Various social networks host student-friendly groups that enable students to connect with each other online before meeting in person to ensure compatibility.

More and more alternative networking sites are designed to match students according to culture, interests, and location. These specific elements will help you find an online buddy in no time!
